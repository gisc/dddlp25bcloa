BQ7: Justify the choice of instructional system design models and the need for the iterative approach.

Guiding Points for Response

Overview of ISD Models

Instructional System Design (ISD) models provide a systematic framework for designing learning experiences.

  • ADDIE (Traditional / Waterfall): The foundational linear model (Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, Evaluate). Best for projects with fixed requirements and low risk of change.
  • Iterative ADDIE: An evolution of ADDIE where each phase is revisited and refined based on evaluation data, rather than moving strictly sequentially.
  • SAM (Successive Approximation Model): An agile model focusing on rapid prototyping and iterative cycles (Savvy Start, Iterative Design, Iterative Development).
  • Dick and Carey Model: A systems-oriented model that views instruction as an entire system, focusing heavily on the interrelationship between context, content, learning, and instruction.
  • Kemp (Morrison, Ross & Kemp) Model: A circular, non-linear model with 9 elements, allowing designers to start at any point. It emphasizes learner characteristics and resources.
  • Knirk and Gustafson Model: A 3-stage model (Problem Determination, Design, Development) useful for small-scale projects or individual lessons.
  • Rapid Prototyping: Focuses on building a working model early to test with users, refining it through spiral cycles.

Why Adopt an Iterative Approach?

  • Risk Mitigation: Identifies errors or misunderstandings early (e.g., during the Design phase rather than after Implementation).
  • Stakeholder Alignment: Continuous feedback loops ensure the product meets the evolving needs of the business and learners.
  • Flexibility: Allows for adjustments based on new data or constraints without scrapping the entire project.

Example: Application of Newer Iterative ADDIE

Scenario: Developing a "Handling Difficult Customers" module for a Call Centre.

Phase Iterative Action
1. Analyze Identified high escalation rates. Iteration: Initial data showed "lack of product knowledge" was a cause, but deeper analysis revealed "lack of empathy skills" was the root cause.
2. Design Drafted a storyboard for a video scenario. Iteration: Showed draft to Team Leads. Feedback: "Too formal." Redesigned to be more casual and colloquial.
3. Develop Created a prototype e-learning module. Iteration: Pilot tested with 5 agents. Found navigation confusing. Fixed UX bugs before full production.
4. Evaluate Conducted throughout (formative). Final Evaluation: Comparing pre- and post-training escalation metrics to inform the next version of the course (e.g., adding a module on chat support).

*Note how "Evaluation" happens at every step, not just the end, driving the next iteration of Design or Development.
